About

Mostly on Sundays, I write about language, learning, and what AI is changing about both, and what that means for the competencies that actually stay with us.

How AI is changing not just what we do, but how we reason, speak, and learn, and what it reveals about the way human thinking actually works.

If AI can generate any content on demand, the learning process itself becomes the real differentiator. Motivation to engage, emotion as a driver, self-direction as a practice, these are the competencies no shortcut replaces.

Technology doesn't only change work. It changes attention, conversation, and the pace at which we think. What do we want to carry forward?

I have a background in linguistics and psychology. In my professional life, that translates into a question I keep returning to: how do people actually learn, and what does AI change about that?

In my work, I follow the same thread: motivation to learn, emotion in the process, and the question of what makes learning self-directed rather than just reactive. What does that look like when AI can produce any piece of content in seconds?

If the content is always available, the process of making sense of it becomes the real competency. That requires knowing how the brain works, how humans think, and what emotions actually do in learning, not as background knowledge, but as the starting point.

My first question about language was visual. My thesis was on how text and image work together in Scandinavian silent film, two systems that don't simply repeat each other, but produce meaning in the space between them. What fascinated me was how the brain handles this: image and language are processed differently, and meaning emerges from how they meet.

Now I watch AI do something similar. Not neurologically, but functionally. Multimodal models are trained on billions of image-text pairs. They don't see or read the way we do, but they've learned what goes together, and they produce something that looks a lot like meaning.

Most people asking questions about AI are asking about capability. I'm more interested in what it reveals about how we learn, and what that means for the competencies worth building.
