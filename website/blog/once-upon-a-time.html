Story

May 23, 2026 · Written By Nicole Schmidbauer

Once upon a time, they lived happily ever after

Over the past few weeks I have taken to noticing, whenever I read about artificial intelligence, the narrative patterns that come up. I have already written about two of them in connection with AGI: AI as the problem solver, and AI as a replacement for the human being.

What has not let go of me since is an observation I want to approach only cautiously, because I cannot really grasp it myself yet. It has to do with the stories that accompany technologies as they come into being. With AI too I have the impression that, however new they may feel, these stories are probably not new at all.

When I think of earlier technologies, of photography, of movie, of television, and later of social media, the same movement of thought seems to repeat itself. First a promise, then a threat, and finally a reassurance meant to calm precisely the thing it names in passing.

Photography, so the story goes, was going to displace painting. Sound film was going to ruin the silent art of the moving image. Television was going to endanger reading and the conversation at the family table. And with social media there was first the great hope of connection, before there was talk of isolation and frayed attention.

The convenient lesson I would rather not draw

I notice in myself how tempting it is to turn this into a soothing moral. It would go: always the same excitement, and in the end things turned out fine. This reading would have the charm of releasing me from any further worry. That is exactly why I do not trust it. It is too smooth for me, and I believe it overlooks what is genuinely interesting.

Because perhaps the repetition is not the thing that matters. Sometimes I wonder whether it is something else instead, a kind of measuring line. If the form of the narrative stays constant across the decades, then it holds a steady line against which whatever deviates becomes visible. Whoever knows what stays the same may find it easier to recognize what is actually changing. Seen this way, the ever same story would not be proof that nothing is happening, but an aid for actually seeing what is happening.

What matters to me in this thought is that this line measures in both directions. It shows me where we are probably ascribing something to technology that has more to do with us than with it. But it shows me just as much where an old worry has come true. Not every fear was imagination. That television would change our attention, that social media could shake the shared ground of facts, was in hindsight not mere projection but remarkably accurate.

So the recurring form does not mean that the content was always unfounded. It seems to me that this is the real value of this historical trace. It helps to distinguish what we project onto a technology from what it actually does, provided one is willing to let both stand side by side.

What might be different about AI

When I place artificial intelligence in front of this steady line, I notice a difference that I still hesitate to make too much of, but that nonetheless will not leave my head.

The earlier technologies were, as I read it, almost always described as doing something to something. Photography multiplied the image, film set it in motion, television broadcast it, social media distributed it. The narrative of replacement was already there too, but it always meant one tool taking the place of another, or one activity displacing another.

With AI we speak differently. We do not say that it transmits or amplifies something. We say that it thinks, that it decides, that it understands. And with that, the old worry about replacement is aimed, perhaps for the first time, not at one skill among many, but at the very faculty with which we would judge the replacement in the first place.

I am not sure whether that is true. But if it is, then it would not be the excitement that is new, but its object. What would be up for debate would not be a tool, but the capacity to judge tools.

A word that says two things

At this point I get caught on a word, as I so often do, and I like it precisely because it carries two meanings at once: performance.

In its first sense it means achievement, the output we deliver. And perhaps that explains why every new technology is brought before the same court, the question of whether it makes us more capable or simply superfluous.

That question belongs, I think, more to us than to the technology. It is the question of a society that is already tuned to compression and yield, and that asks of everything new, first of all, what it means for its output. The technology would then be the surface, and the worry about performance would be us. That AI of all things meets a present already so compressed might explain why it is received so readily as either a final relief or a final devaluation.

In its second sense, performance means something that interests me from a linguistic point of view, the enacting quality of language. For the talk of performance does not only describe whether AI makes us more productive. It also presupposes that productivity is the right measure. Whoever speaks first of relief or of replacement has, at bottom, already conceded that the matter is one of performance.

The judgment sits in the frame, before the argument even begins.

This is the same observation that has occupied me before, only this time not in a single term, but in a whole narrative form.

Where this leads me

With all of this I do not want to make a diagnosis; the subject feels too unfinished for me for that. What remains for me at the end of this reflection is more a shift of attention.

The historical trace can show me why the question of performance is so obvious and so hard to avoid. What it cannot take from me is the choice of whether I make that question the only one.

And it is right here that the point at which I can still decide something becomes smaller and more concrete than the grand word of discourse would suggest. For I am not always aware of how the terms I reach for myself transform reality.
