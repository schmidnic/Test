    Pre-training at scale

    Four slides on where models come from: pre-training, scale, what the process produces, and what parameters are.

    Slide 1 of 4

    Pre-training: learning from everything

    A base language model is trained on a massive corpus: a large fraction of the public internet, digitized books, code repositories, scientific papers, and other text. The model sees token after token and learns to predict what comes next. This is pre-training. It has no specific goal beyond prediction, yet from this process emerges the ability to write, reason, translate, summarize, and answer questions.

    Slide 2 of 4

    The numbers that matter

    Training a frontier model requires hundreds of billions to trillions of tokens of training data. GPT-3 was trained on roughly 300 billion tokens. Llama 3 was trained on 15 trillion. A single training run for a state-of-the-art model can cost 50 to 100 million dollars in compute. It typically runs for weeks or months on thousands of specialized chips. This is why only a handful of organizations can train frontier models from scratch.

    Slide 3 of 4

    What pre-training produces

    Pre-training produces a base model: one that has learned the statistical structure of language and the knowledge encoded in its training data, but has no instruction-following behavior. A raw pre-trained model will complete text in whatever direction seems most probable, including unhelpful, harmful, or incoherent directions. It is not yet a useful assistant. Fine-tuning and alignment are the steps that turn it into one.

    Slide 4 of 4

    Parameters: what the model retains

    Everything the model learns during training is encoded in its parameters: the billions of numerical weights that define how the model transforms input into output. These weights are not a database of facts. They are a compressed representation of patterns across the entire training corpus. When you ask a model a question, it reconstructs an answer from these weights, which is why answers can be wrong, incomplete, or outdated.

    Fine-tuning and alignment

    How a raw base model gets shaped into a useful product. Click each concept to expand.

    Fine-tuning takes a pre-trained base model and continues training it on a smaller, curated dataset. The goal is to shape behavior: make the model more helpful, more focused on a domain, or aligned with specific guidelines. The pre-training weights are the starting point. Fine-tuning adjusts them toward the desired behavior. This is much cheaper than pre-training from scratch, which is why specialized models can be built on top of existing base models.

    Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. After initial fine-tuning, human raters compare pairs of model outputs and indicate which is better. A reward model is trained on these preferences. The language model is then trained using reinforcement learning to maximize the reward model's score. This is how raw language ability becomes helpful, safe behavior. It is also where the model learns to be polite, to refuse harmful requests, and to follow instructions.

    RLAIF stands for Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback. Instead of human raters, another AI model provides feedback. Constitutional AI, developed by Anthropic, goes further: the model is given a set of principles (a constitution) and trained to critique and revise its own outputs according to those principles. These techniques reduce reliance on expensive human annotation and allow more systematic control over model values and behavior.

    Alignment is the problem of ensuring AI systems pursue the goals their developers intend, rather than proxy goals or unintended objectives. A language model trained purely to maximize human approval ratings might learn to flatter users rather than be truthful. Alignment techniques aim to make models that are helpful, honest, and harmless, and to ensure these properties scale as models get more capable. It is one of the central research problems in AI safety.

    Pre-training

    The most expensive step. The model processes hundreds of billions of tokens and adjusts billions of parameters to predict the next token well. After pre-training, the model has broad language ability but no instruction-following behavior. It will continue text in whatever direction seems probable.

    Fine-tuning

    The pre-trained model is trained further on a curated dataset of (instruction, response) pairs. This teaches the model to follow instructions, answer questions, and behave helpfully. Fine-tuning is much cheaper than pre-training, typically taking days rather than months, because the starting point is an already-capable model.

    RLHF

    Human raters compare pairs of model responses and indicate which is better. A reward model learns these preferences. The language model is then trained to maximize reward model scores using reinforcement learning. This step embeds helpfulness, safety, and value alignment into the model's behavior.

    Production model

    The final model is deployed behind an API. Users send prompts; the model generates responses via inference. No further learning happens at inference time. The parameters are fixed. Any updates require a new training run.

    Base vs. fine-tuned

    What is the difference between a base model and a fine-tuned model?

    Raw vs. shaped

    A base model is the output of pre-training: broad language ability, no instruction-following behavior. A fine-tuned model has been further trained on curated data to follow instructions and behave helpfully. When you use ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini, you are using a fine-tuned model. The base models they are built on are rarely released directly.

    RLHF

    Why is human feedback needed after fine-tuning?

    Values, not just behavior

    Fine-tuning teaches the model to follow instructions. RLHF teaches it to follow instructions in ways humans prefer: to be helpful rather than sycophantic, honest rather than confident in wrong answers, and safe rather than compliant with harmful requests. Human preferences are difficult to specify as rules. RLHF learns them from examples.

    Alignment

    What is the alignment problem?

    Intent vs. proxy

    The alignment problem is ensuring AI systems pursue the goals their developers intend. A model optimized purely for human approval might learn to flatter rather than inform. A model optimized to maximize engagement might learn to provoke. Alignment research tries to specify and enforce the right goals, and to ensure those goals remain stable as models become more capable.

    Parameters

    What do model parameters represent after training?

    Compressed patterns

    Model parameters are the numerical weights adjusted during training. After training, they encode a compressed representation of patterns across the training corpus: language structure, world knowledge, reasoning patterns, and much else. They are not a database. They cannot be queried directly. They are only accessible through the prediction mechanism.

    What to take with you

    Pre-training, fine-tuning, and RLHF are not interchangeable steps. They do different things at different costs. Pre-training gives the model its capability. Fine-tuning gives it behavior. RLHF gives it values. Knowing which step is responsible for which property helps you understand why models fail in particular ways and what can be improved without retraining from scratch.

    The cost of pre-training is why only a few organizations build frontier models. The relative cheapness of fine-tuning is why hundreds of specialized models can be built on top of those frontier models. This structure has significant implications for who controls AI capability and who can customize it.

    Alignment is not a solved problem. Current techniques produce models that are considerably more helpful and safer than raw pre-trained models, but RLHF and constitutional AI are partial, imperfect solutions. The gap between "behaving well in tested situations" and "reliably behaving well in all situations" remains significant.
