How language models work

1 / 8

    Tokens and prediction

    Four slides on how language models process text: tokenization, context, prediction, and why things go wrong.

    Slide 1 of 4

    Tokens, not words

    Language models do not process words. They process tokens: fragments of text, roughly the size of a syllable or short word. The word 'understanding' becomes something like 'under,' 'stand,' 'ing.' A sentence of 10 words might be 12 to 15 tokens. The model never sees letters directly. It sees a sequence of integer IDs, each representing a token, drawn from a fixed vocabulary of roughly 50,000 to 100,000 entries.

    Slide 2 of 4

    One thing, done very fast

    A language model does one thing: it predicts the next token. Given everything so far, it assigns a probability to every token in its vocabulary and picks one. Then it does it again with the new token appended. And again. A response that feels like thinking is a cascade of next-token predictions, each conditioned on all previous tokens. The model generates one token at a time, left to right, with no ability to revise what it has already written.

    Slide 3 of 4

    Where the 'knowledge' comes from

    The model's parameters encode statistical patterns over tokens. During training, the model saw enormous amounts of text and adjusted its weights to predict the next token well. The result is that the weights implicitly encode information about language, facts, reasoning patterns, and much else. But this knowledge is not stored as retrievable facts. It is distributed across billions of weights and can only be accessed through the prediction mechanism.

    Slide 4 of 4

    Why hallucination is structural

    A language model optimizes for predicting likely next tokens, not for truthfulness. It produces text that is statistically plausible given its training data. When a question falls outside the reliable patterns in that data, the model still predicts a plausible-sounding answer, because that is what it does. Hallucination is not a bug to be patched. It is the direct consequence of the prediction mechanism applied in domains where training data was sparse or wrong.

    Inside the model

    The four mechanisms that define how a language model works. Click each to expand.

    Attention is the mechanism that allows a language model to weigh how relevant each previous token is when predicting the next one. For each token being predicted, the model computes a query (what am I looking for?), keys (what does each previous token offer?), and values (what does each token contribute?). Attention scores are dot products of queries and keys. Higher scores mean more influence on the prediction. This is where the O(n²) cost comes from: every token attends to every other token.

    An embedding is a dense numerical representation of a token (or a word, sentence, or concept). In a high-dimensional space, semantically similar items are placed near each other. 'Dog' and 'puppy' have similar embeddings. 'Dog' and 'accountant' do not. Models learn embeddings during training. They are the bridge between discrete tokens (IDs) and the continuous mathematical space the transformer operates in. Embeddings are also used independently for search and retrieval.

    Temperature controls how the model samples from its probability distribution when generating the next token. At temperature 0, the model always picks the most probable token (deterministic, repetitive). At higher temperatures, it samples from a broader distribution, producing more varied and creative outputs, but also more likely to go off-track. Most production systems use a temperature between 0.5 and 1.0. Temperature is one reason the same prompt can produce different outputs on different runs.

    Hallucination occurs when a model generates text that sounds plausible but is factually wrong. The causes are structural: the model predicts likely tokens, not true facts. When the correct answer is underrepresented in training data, or when the model is asked about events after its training cutoff, it fills the gap with statistically plausible text. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and grounding techniques reduce hallucination by giving the model accurate context, but cannot eliminate it.

    Tokenization

    The raw text is split into tokens: subword units from a fixed vocabulary. Common words are single tokens. Rare words are split into multiple tokens. This step determines how the model 'sees' your input. The total number of tokens affects both cost and context window usage.

    Embeddings

    Each token ID is mapped to a dense vector of numbers (typically 768 to 12,288 dimensions depending on model size). These vectors encode the token's meaning in a high-dimensional space. Semantically related tokens have similar vectors. The embedding layer is learned during training.

    Transformer layers

    The core of the model. Multiple layers of attention and feed-forward networks process the token embeddings. Each layer refines the representation. Attention allows each token to gather context from all other tokens in the sequence. This is the computationally expensive step.

    Output: next token

    The final layer produces a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary. The model picks the next token by sampling from this distribution. The chosen token is appended to the sequence, and the whole process repeats for the next prediction.

    Tokens

    Why does tokenization matter for how models process text?

    Fragments, not words

    Tokenization determines how the model sees input. Rare words get split into multiple tokens, costing more context and sometimes losing semantic coherence. The same sentence tokenized differently can produce different model behavior. Understanding tokens helps predict model performance on unusual words or non-English text.

    Embeddings

    What is the difference between a token ID and a token embedding?

    Integer vs. vector

    A token ID is a simple integer from the vocabulary index, e.g., 4,521. A token embedding is a dense vector of hundreds or thousands of numbers representing the token's meaning. The embedding layer translates IDs into vectors the transformer can compute with. Embeddings encode semantic relationships: similar tokens are close in embedding space.

    Attention

    What problem does the attention mechanism solve?

    Contextual relevance

    Without attention, a model would treat each token in isolation. Attention allows every token to gather context from all other tokens, weighting them by relevance. The word 'bank' in 'river bank' attends heavily to 'river.' The same word in 'bank transfer' attends to 'transfer.' Attention is how language models handle context and ambiguity.

    Hallucination

    Why can a language model state confidently that something false is true?

    Plausibility over truth

    The model predicts the statistically most likely next token, not the true next token. Confidence in the output reflects confidence in the prediction, not in factual accuracy. A model trained on text that often paired authoritative tone with declarative statements will reproduce that pattern even when the content is wrong.

    What to take with you

    A language model is a next-token predictor. Every response, however sophisticated it appears, is the result of this single operation repeated thousands of times. That framing is not reductive. It is precise. And precision is what allows you to predict when models will perform well and when they will fail.

    Hallucination is not a reliability problem to be patched. It is a structural consequence of the prediction mechanism. The model is not lying. It is producing statistically plausible text. When the plausible text happens to be false, that is hallucination. Grounding the model in retrieved facts reduces the problem but cannot eliminate it.

    Tokens, embeddings, and attention are not jargon. They are the three mechanisms that convert your text into a model output. Understanding them tells you why models struggle with rare words, why context length costs money, and why the same prompt can produce different outputs on different runs.
