    Software that learns

    Four slides that build the conceptual foundation: what AI is, what a model is, how it learns, and what it cannot do.

    Slide 1 of 4

    Software that learns

    Traditional software follows rules a programmer wrote. If a user does X, the program does Y. Every possible case must be anticipated and handled. The rules are explicit, readable, and fixed. AI works differently: instead of rules, you give it examples. The model adjusts itself until it gets better at predicting the right outcome. No one writes a rule for every case. The rules emerge from the data.

    Slide 2 of 4

    What a model actually is

    A model is a mathematical function with parameters: billions of numerical weights that get adjusted during training. When you show the model an example, it makes a prediction. The prediction is compared to the correct answer. The difference, the error, is used to nudge the weights slightly in the right direction. Repeated billions of times, the weights converge on patterns that generalize.

    Slide 3 of 4

    Three kinds of learning

    Supervised learning: the model learns from labeled examples (input + correct output). Unsupervised learning: the model finds structure in unlabeled data. Reinforcement learning: the model learns by taking actions and receiving rewards or penalties. Most of the AI you interact with, including all large language models, is trained using a combination of supervised learning and reinforcement learning from human feedback.

    Slide 4 of 4

    What AI is not

    AI is not consciousness. It is not a thinking agent with intentions. It is not searching a database of facts. A language model does not understand what you write the way you do. It predicts what should come next, based on patterns absorbed during training. That distinction matters most when things go wrong, and understanding it is the foundation for using these systems well.

    Key concepts

    Four terms you will encounter constantly in AI conversations. Click each one to expand the definition.

    Machine learning is the branch of AI where systems learn from data rather than following explicit rules. A machine learning model is trained on examples and adjusts its internal parameters to improve performance on a task. The key insight: you define the goal, provide data, and let the training process find the solution. This is fundamentally different from classical programming, where the programmer defines every step.

    Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that uses neural networks with many layers. Each layer transforms its input into a more abstract representation. The 'depth' refers to the number of layers. Deep learning became dominant around 2012 when GPUs made training large networks feasible. Almost all modern AI, including language models, image generators, and speech recognition, uses deep learning.

    A neural network is a computational structure loosely inspired by the brain. It consists of layers of nodes (neurons), each connected to nodes in adjacent layers by weighted edges. During training, the weights are adjusted so the network produces correct outputs. The architecture determines how information flows. Transformer models, the basis of most language models, are a specific neural network architecture introduced in 2017.

    Training data is the set of examples a model learns from. Its quality, diversity, and scale largely determine what the model can do. A language model trained on internet text learns to mimic the patterns of internet text: its knowledge, its biases, its blind spots. Garbage in, garbage out is a cliché that happens to be accurate. Many problems with AI systems, including hallucination and bias, trace back to issues in the training data.

    Artificial Intelligence

    The broad field of systems that perform tasks requiring human-like intelligence. AI encompasses perception, reasoning, language, and decision-making. Machine learning is the dominant approach today, but AI also includes symbolic reasoning, expert systems, and other paradigms.

    Supervised Learning

    The model learns from labeled examples: input paired with the correct output. Most language model training starts here. You show the model thousands of (prompt, ideal response) pairs and train it to reproduce the pattern. The quality of the labels matters enormously.

    Deep Learning

    Neural networks with many layers. Each layer learns increasingly abstract representations of the input. Deep learning is why AI became viable for language, images, and speech. The word 'deep' just means many layers, not sophistication in any philosophical sense.

    Large Language Models

    A specific type of deep learning model trained on vast amounts of text to predict the next token. GPT, Claude, Gemini, and Llama are all LLMs. They are the current dominant paradigm for language AI and the basis for the systems most people interact with today.

    Concepts

    What is the difference between AI, machine learning, and deep learning?

    Nested fields

    AI is the broadest category: systems that perform intelligent tasks. Machine learning is a subset: systems that learn from data. Deep learning is a subset of machine learning: neural networks with many layers. Most of what is called AI today is deep learning.

    Types

    What distinguishes supervised from unsupervised learning?

    Labels

    Supervised learning uses labeled data: the model learns from (input, correct output) pairs. Unsupervised learning finds structure in unlabeled data. Supervised learning powers most language model training. Unsupervised techniques are used to learn representations and cluster data.

    Scope

    What is narrow AI, and does general AI exist?

    Narrow, not general

    Every AI system that exists today is narrow: it does one kind of thing well. Narrow AI can outperform humans on specific tasks (chess, protein folding, text generation) but cannot transfer that ability to other domains. Artificial general intelligence, a system that can reason across arbitrary domains, does not exist.

    Models

    What is a generative AI model?

    Generating new content

    Generative AI produces new content rather than classifying existing content. Language models generate text. Image models generate images. Audio models generate speech or music. The key capability: the model learns a distribution over possible outputs and samples from it. Large language models are a specific type of generative model.

    What to take with you

    AI is not magic and not consciousness. It is a statistical function trained on data. That framing is the most useful lens for understanding both its capabilities and its failures. When a model performs impressively, it is because the training data contained patterns that generalized. When it fails, it is because the distribution shifted or the data contained gaps.

    The distinction between narrow and general AI matters practically. Every tool you use today is narrow. It is optimized for a specific task and can fail in unexpected ways outside that task. Treating AI as narrow, not general, is both more accurate and more useful.

    Machine learning, deep learning, and large language models are nested concepts, not interchangeable synonyms. Knowing the difference allows you to follow technical coverage more precisely and ask better questions when things go wrong.
