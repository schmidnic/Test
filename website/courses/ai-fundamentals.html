    What AI actually is

    Use the arrows to move through the four slides.

    Slide 1 of 4

    Software that learns

    Traditional software follows rules a programmer wrote. If a user does X, the program does Y. AI works differently: instead of rules, you give it examples. The model adjusts itself until it gets better at predicting the right outcome. Nobody writes a rule for every case. The rules emerge from the data.

    Slide 2 of 4

    What "learning" means here

    When a model learns, it adjusts billions of internal numerical values called parameters. Each adjustment is a tiny response to being wrong. Done across millions of examples, the model gradually gets better. There is no insight, no understanding, no moment of recognition. Just arithmetic, at enormous scale.

    Slide 3 of 4

    Not the robots from the movies

    AI is not a thinking agent with goals and intentions. It is a function: something goes in, something comes out. A language model does not understand what you write. It predicts what should come next, based on patterns absorbed during training. That distinction matters the moment things go wrong.

    Slide 4 of 4

    Narrow, not general

    Every AI system that exists today is narrow: it does one kind of thing well. ChatGPT generates text. Midjourney generates images. AlphaFold predicts protein structures. What they share: each was trained on specific data for a specific task. Artificial general intelligence, the kind from science fiction, does not exist.

    How language models work

    Use the arrows to move through the four slides.

    Slide 1 of 4

    Tokens, not words

    Language models do not process words. They process tokens: fragments of text, roughly the size of a syllable or short word. The word "understanding" becomes something like "under," "stand," "ing." The model sees a sequence of these fragments, not sentences the way you read them.

    Slide 2 of 4

    The next token game

    A language model does one thing: it predicts the next token. Given everything so far, it assigns probabilities to every possible next token and picks one. Then it does it again. And again. What feels like a conversation is a very fast, very large sequence of these predictions.

    Slide 3 of 4

    Where the knowledge lives

    The knowledge a language model has is encoded in its parameters: billions of numerical weights, adjusted during training. There is no database being searched. There is no fact-checker running in the background. When a model answers a question, it reconstructs an answer from learned distributions. That is why hallucination is not a bug to be fixed. It is a structural feature of how these systems work.

    Slide 4 of 4

    Fluency is not accuracy

    A language model optimizes for the next token being likely given everything that came before. That produces text that sounds natural and coherent. It does not guarantee that the content is true. The gap between fluency and accuracy is the most important thing to keep in mind when using these systems.

    How models get built

    Three terms that appear constantly in AI coverage. Click each to understand the distinction.

    A base model is trained on a massive dataset: a large fraction of the public internet, plus books, code, and other text. The model sees a token, predicts the next one, gets feedback on whether it was right, and adjusts. Done across billions of examples, the parameters gradually converge. Training a frontier model costs tens of millions of dollars and takes months. It happens once.

    Fine-tuning takes a trained base model and trains it further on a smaller, more specific dataset. This shapes its behavior: makes it more helpful, more focused on a domain, or aligned with particular guidelines. GPT, Claude, and Gemini are all fine-tuned versions of base models. Fine-tuning is much cheaper than training from scratch, but the base model must already exist.

    Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. After initial training, humans rate model outputs: this response is better than that one. The model is then trained to produce responses humans prefer. This is how raw language models become assistants. It is also where values and guardrails get embedded, which makes it one of the most consequential steps in the entire process.

    Inference is when the model runs: you send a prompt, it generates a response. No learning happens during inference. The model's parameters are fixed. This is what you interact with every time you use a language model. Inference costs much less than training, but at scale, billions of queries per day, it remains significant.

    Context, memory, and limits

    Use the arrows to move through the four slides.

    Slide 1 of 4

    The context window

    Every language model has a context window: the maximum amount of text it can see at once. Anything outside the window does not exist for the model. It cannot recall it, reference it, or reason about it. The context window is not RAM. It is more like a whiteboard with a hard size limit.

    Slide 2 of 4

    Memory is the wrong word

    When a model seems to remember something from earlier in a conversation, it is because that earlier text is still in the context window. When the conversation grows too long, or when you start a new session, that text is gone. The model has no persistent memory by default. It does not know you from last time.

    Slide 3 of 4

    What this limits in practice

    A model cannot read an entire book in one pass. It cannot reason over a full year of emails. It cannot track a project across weeks without external storage. Every AI assistant that appears to have memory is using something else: a database, a summarization pipeline, or a retrieval system. The model itself forgets the moment the window closes.

    Slide 4 of 4

    Context lengths are growing fast

    Early GPT models had context windows of 4,000 tokens, roughly a few pages. Today, Claude and Gemini support 1 million tokens and above. Larger context windows do not eliminate the memory problem entirely, but they push the boundary significantly outward. What you can do with a 1 million token window is fundamentally different from what was possible two years ago.

    Models and the people who build them

    The players

    Who builds the models you interact with?

    The main labs

    OpenAI (GPT), Anthropic (Claude), Google (Gemini), Meta (Llama, open source), Mistral (open source, European). Each takes a different approach to openness, safety, and deployment.

    Access

    What is the difference between open and closed source models?

    Control and infrastructure

    Closed models (GPT, Claude, Gemini) run on the provider's servers, accessed via API. Open source models (Llama, Mistral) can be downloaded and run locally. Open source enables full control but requires infrastructure.

    Foundation

    What is a foundation model?

    A trained starting point

    A large base model trained on broad data, capable of many tasks. GPT, Claude, and Gemini are all foundation models. They serve as the base for more specialized applications through fine-tuning.

    Size

    What does "model size" actually measure?

    Parameters

    Model size is measured in parameters: the numerical weights adjusted during training. GPT-3 had 175 billion. Larger models generally perform better but cost more to run. Training data quality and fine-tuning matter as much as size alone.

    What comes after the chat

    The shift from chatbot to agent is the most consequential change currently underway in how AI gets deployed. Click each concept to understand it.

    A prompt is the input you give a model: a question, an instruction, or a context. Prompting is both the act of doing this and a growing discipline of doing it well. The same model can produce very different outputs depending on how the prompt is structured. This is why prompt engineering became a job title, and why it will likely stop being one once models get better at interpreting intent.

    Language models can be connected to external tools: a web search, a calculator, a code interpreter, a database. When a model uses a tool, it generates an instruction, the tool executes it, and the result comes back into the context window. The model responds using the result. This extends what models can do significantly beyond generating text from memory.

    An agent is a model that takes a goal, breaks it into steps, and executes those steps autonomously using available tools, sometimes calling other models. Agents do not wait for a human to approve each step. They plan, act, check results, and adjust. This is the dominant direction of AI development in 2025 and 2026.

    When AI is autonomous, the failure modes change. A chatbot gives a bad answer. An agent takes a wrong action. The difference between answering incorrectly and acting incorrectly is significant. Understanding what agents can and cannot yet do reliably is the most practically relevant knowledge for anyone working with or near AI systems right now.
